Photosynthesis

Abstract:

Photosynthesis is like a magic trick that plants can do to make their own food. Just like humans need food to grow and stay healthy, plants also need food to survive. However, instead of going to the grocery store, plants have a special power called photosynthesis. This ability helps them make their own food using sunlight, water, and air. Humans also benefit from photosynthesis because the process produces a natural gas that we need to breathe; oxygen! Let's learn about the process of photosynthesis in more detail.

Notice that the first product in the equation is glucose. Glucose is a type of sugar. In photosynthesis, glucose is the food that plants create. Along with glucose, oxygen is another product. We briefly talked about both of these products previously.

.1 Light Reaction

There are two stages in photosynthesis; the Light dependent reaction and the light independent reaction. It's important to note that the light independent reaction is often called the “Calvin cycle”. For now, let's look at the light dependent reaction.


The light dependent reaction occurs in the thylakoid membrane of the chloroplast. Light and water are taken in and what's produced is ATP and NADPH. These products get passed on to the next step…

.2 Light independent Reaction

The light dependent reaction happens in the stroma of the chloroplast. Carbon dioxide is taken in through pores in the plant's leaves called stomata. The ATP from the light independent reaction is used as a power supply for this cycle. The ATP, NADPH, and CO2 are finally converted into glucose.

Respiration

Abstract:

Now that you understand photosynthesis, it's time to learn about respiration. Imagine our cells as tiny factories that need fuel to keep running smoothly. When we eat food, our bodies break it down into sugar (glucose). This sugar then goes into the powerhouse of our cells called mitochondria. Inside the mitochondria, the sugar combines with the oxygen we breathe in to create energy in the form of ATP.

Equation:

The respiration equation is located on the left.

Notice that the inputs of this equation where the outputs of the last equation. How cool is that! We previously talked about how plants make glucose but we haven't yet talked about what they do with it once it's made. This section will explain how plants and humans make energy within their cells.

.1 Glycolysis

There are three steps in the process of cellular respiration:

  • Glycolysis
  • Krebs Cycle
  • Electron Transport Chain


For now, let's discuss glycolysis. Glycolysis is the first step in the process of cellular respiration. It occurs in the cytoplasm of the cell. The process takes glucose and converts it into a more useful form called pyruvic. In addition to pyruvic, 4 ATP molecules and 2 NADH are also produced as products of glycolysis.

Shortly after, the two pyruvic are transported into the mitochondrial matrix where they become oxidized. There, they will be converted from pyrivic to acetyl CoA. It's important to note that carbon dioxide is also released and passed on to the next step...

.2 The Krebs Cycle

The Krebs cycle ( also known as the "Citric Acid Cycle" ) is a closed-loop process that occurs in the mitochondrial matrix. It is quite complicated so here is a simple rundown:

  1. The acetyl CoA from the previous step enters the cycle
  2. The outputs include: 2 ATP, 6 NADH, and 2 FADH

.3 Electron Transport Chain

The electron transport chain is the last step in the process of cellular respiration. This step takes place in the inner mitochondrial membrane. It's important to note that this step is very complex and we are greatly simplifying it.

Here's the rundown:

  • electrons are transferred from the NADH and the FADH to protein carriers
  • These electrons create a gradient that pumps hydrogen atoms up into the outer mitochondrial membrane ( intermembrane space )
  • The surplus of protons in the outer membrane space is forced back down into the inner membrane space through a channel protein called ATP synthase. This process creates an abundance of ATP!
  • The electron from earlier eventually makes its way through the protein carriers and bonds with hydrogen atoms to make water.

Guide Complete!

Congratulations, you've completed this guide! Here is a quick summary of what you just learned:

Photosynthesis

  • Photosynthesis allows plants to create their own food
  • The photosynthesis equation is: carbon dioxide + water = glucose and oxygen
  • The light reaction is the first step in photosynthesis. It occurs in the thylakoid membrane. Light and water are taken in, ATP and NADPH are produced.
  • The light independent reaction occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast. Carbon dioxide is taken in through the pores. The ATP, NADH, and Co2 are converted into glucose

Respiration

  • Respiration is a cellular process that produces energy in the form of ATP
  • The cellular respiration equation is: Glucose + Oxygen = Carbon Dioxide and Water
  • Glycolysis is the first step in respiration. It takes a glucose molecule and converts it into 2 pyruvic
  • There is an intermediate step between glycolysis and the Krebs cycle. In this step, the 2 pyruvic are converted into acetyl CoA, and oxygen is also released.
  • The Krebs cycle takes the outputs of the previous step and produces 2 ATP, 6 NADH, and 2 FADH.
  • The electron transport chain is the last step in cellular respiration. First, electrons are transported from the NADH and FADH to protein carriers.
  • Next hydrogen atoms are pumped from the mitochondrial matrix to the inner membrane space and then back through ATP synthase to produce ATP energy.
  • Finally, the electrons make their way through the carrier proteins and bond with hydrogen atoms to create water.
